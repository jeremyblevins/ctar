# Cyber Threat Assessment and Reporting (CTAR) Framework

## Making Cyber Threat Intelligence Understandable

The **Cyber Threat Assessment and Reporting (CTAR) Framework** is an open educational framework that helps students, educators, researchers, and cybersecurity practitioners understand cyber threat intelligence through structured, evidence-based analysis.

CTAR brings together established concepts from cybersecurity, intelligence analysis, research methodology, and education into a practical framework for investigating cyber incidents, evaluating evidence, and communicating analytical conclusions.

Rather than teaching people *what to think*, CTAR teaches **how to think critically about cyber threats**.

**Vision**

> **To make cyber threat intelligence accessible, transparent, and teachable for everyone.**

---

# Why CTAR?

Cybersecurity is filled with headlines, technical reports, vendor research, government advisories, and social media commentary. While these sources often provide valuable information, they rarely explain **how conclusions were reached**, **how reliable the evidence is**, or **what uncertainty remains**.

CTAR was created to help bridge that gap.

The framework encourages learners to ask questions such as:

* What evidence supports this claim?
* Where did this information originate?
* How reliable are the available sources?
* What assumptions are being made?
* What alternative explanations exist?
* How confident should we be in our conclusions?

These questions are fundamental not only to cyber threat intelligence, but also to incident response, governance, risk management, digital forensics, cybersecurity research, and informed decision making.

# Use Cases

The CTAR Framework supports multiple approaches to learning and investigation.

## Learn from Cybersecurity News

Start with a news article from a trusted source and build a deeper understanding through additional research, source evaluation, technical context, and structured analysis.

---

## Research Historical Cyber Incidents

Investigate significant cyber events using publicly available information.

Examples include:

* ILOVEYOU
* Morris Worm
* Stuxnet
* WannaCry
* NotPetya
* SolarWinds
* Colonial Pipeline
* Log4Shell

Rather than reading a summary, learners investigate the evidence and construct their own assessment.

---

## Conduct Independent Research

Begin with a topic, vulnerability, malware family, threat actor, or campaign and build an evidence-based assessment from authoritative open sources.

CTAR provides a repeatable workflow for organizing research and documenting analytical reasoning.

---

## Classroom Learning

CTAR supports inquiry-based learning by replacing passive reading with active investigation.

Students learn to:

* evaluate evidence
* compare sources
* identify assumptions
* communicate uncertainty
* produce structured analytical reports

The goal is to develop analytical reasoning that transfers to professional cybersecurity practice.

---

## Professional Research

Cybersecurity practitioners can use CTAR as a structured framework for organizing evidence, documenting analytical reasoning, and communicating findings during independent research.

The framework emphasizes transparency, reproducibility, and clearly documented confidence assessments.

---

# Guiding Principles

The CTAR Framework is built upon several core principles.

## Evidence First

Analytical conclusions should be supported by verifiable evidence whenever possible.

---

## Transparency

Facts, assumptions, analysis, confidence, and uncertainty should be clearly distinguished.

---

## Critical Thinking

CTAR encourages questioning, corroboration, and consideration of alternative explanations rather than acceptance of unsupported conclusions.

---

## Education

The framework is designed to help learners understand the analytical process behind cyber threat intelligence.

---

## AI Independence

CTAR is intentionally AI-agnostic.

The framework is published using open formats and may be used with any capable artificial intelligence system or entirely without AI. The methodology defines the analytical process; AI is an optional tool that can assist in applying it.

---

## Open Knowledge

CTAR is an open educational project centered on shared knowledge, transparent methodology, and community contribution.

---

# AI Collaboration

The CTAR Framework was developed through an iterative collaboration between its founder and generative artificial intelligence.

Rather than being generated from a single prompt, the framework evolved through ongoing discussion, refinement, experimentation, and critical evaluation. Human judgment guided the educational philosophy, methodology, and final decisions, while AI served as a collaborative partner for brainstorming, drafting, editing, and exploring alternatives.

This commitment to transparency reflects one of CTAR's central values: readers should understand not only analytical conclusions, but also how those conclusions were developed.

---

# Open Project

The CTAR Project is an open educational initiative dedicated to improving cybersecurity education through transparent, evidence-based analytical practices.

The project welcomes contributions from:

* educators
* cybersecurity practitioners
* students
* researchers
* technical writers
* software developers

Contributions may include methodology improvements, educational materials, historical case studies, example assessments, software tools, documentation, and translations.

---

# What CTAR Is Not

CTAR is **not**:

* a commercial threat intelligence platform
* an attribution authority
* a replacement for professional intelligence tradecraft
* an autonomous AI analyst
* a cybersecurity product

Instead, CTAR is an educational framework that teaches structured analytical reasoning through evidence-based investigation.
